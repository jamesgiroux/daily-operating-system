# What OpenClaw, GBrain, Obsidian, and personal-context directories taught us

**Status:** stub. Drafting TBD.

## Synopsis

DailyOS did not emerge in a vacuum. OpenClaw, GBrain, Obsidian workflows, Karpathy-style personal context directories, and a broader wave of "AI that knows my work" experiments all validated parts of the direction. They also clarified what DailyOS should not become. This entry is about the overlap, the differences, and the sharper product thesis that came out of studying them.

## Outline

- The lived moment: seeing other people independently converge on memory, retrieval, and operational context as the real problem.
- What they validated: persistent context matters, hybrid retrieval matters, the harness matters, and chat alone is not enough.
- What they did differently: engineer-first interaction, prompt-heavy flows, flatter memory models, or weaker boundaries around trust and privacy.
- What DailyOS kept: retrieval lessons, memory lessons, MCP openness, and the proof that this category is real.
- What DailyOS rejected: chat as the primary interface, shallow integration breadth, and the assumption that the user should always know what to ask.
- What's still open: which patterns are universal, and which are specific to a TAM / Customer Success wedge.
