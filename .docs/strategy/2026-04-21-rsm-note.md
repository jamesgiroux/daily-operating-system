# A note on AI, work, and what I've been building

**By James Giroux. For Automattic RSM consideration.**

Every morning I sit down to work and rebuild the same mental model I had yesterday. Who's on my calendar. Who I promised what. Which accounts are showing trouble signs. Who's gone quiet on me. Which thread I should pick up first.

I'm a CSM, so the model is accounts and stakeholders and commitments. But I've watched enough other people work to know the pattern is the same for everyone. Project leads rebuild who's blocked on what. Engineers rebuild what's been tried on a bug. Designers rebuild which stakeholder cares about which product direction. The mental model gets reconstructed every morning, held loosely during the day, and mostly lost by the time I context-switch at 3pm.

I've been using AI tools to help, and they do, a little. But every one of them starts from zero. I type the same context back in at the top of every new chat. "I'm a CSM. Here's the account. Here's what we discussed last month." The AI reads, replies, forgets by tomorrow. I'm still the integration layer. The AI is faster typing and better spelling and a second opinion, but the job of holding the world together is still mine.

## The side project

Since the last week of January, about three months ago, I've been building a side project called DailyOS to see whether it's possible to build something different. Something that was already paying attention. Something where the first thing I saw in the morning was a briefing about my day, assembled while I slept, with confidence bands and clickable sources. Something where if I corrected a fact, the correction stayed corrected.

Three months is a short time to have built what's there. That's the story of AI velocity right now: what used to take a year compresses into a season, if the tools are good and the problem is clear. It's also why the code is uneven and the UI is functional at best. I keep breaking things for days at a time while I rewrite the pieces underneath. It is a working prototype I use every morning and a long set of mistakes I've been trying to avoid repeating.

Here's what I've come to believe from building it:

**The AI isn't the hard part.** Models are already good enough for almost anything I throw at them. What's hard is everything around the model. Where do I store what the AI learns so it doesn't evaporate between sessions? How do I know which of its claims to trust? How do I let the user say "no, you were wrong about that" and have the correction actually stick, instead of silently reverting the next time the AI runs?

**Most products skip the hard part.** Every AI-for-work tool I've tried, paid or free, hand-waves these. They generate confidently, forget by the next session, quietly paper over user corrections. It works for a while and then trust dies, usually around week three. You open the tool, see something wrong, and start wondering what else is wrong. Then you stop opening it.

**Privacy turned out to be a design constraint, not a feature.** I work at Automattic. I can't build something that pipes my email and meeting transcripts and account notes to a vendor's server. So DailyOS has been local-first from day one. Content doesn't leave my laptop. The LLM call uses my own key. Any server component, if one ever exists, sees metadata, not content. This wasn't a checkbox I added at the end; it's a constraint that forced harder decisions at every layer. It made a lot of the architecture harder. It also, I think, made it better.

## I am not alone in this

Andrej Karpathy published an LLM Wiki gist last week that described much of what I'd been trying to do. I read the comments and found myself nodding at the problems people were surfacing, thinking "I've already had to solve that" on most of them. Not because I'm smart, because I've been living with the problem longer and each time something broke I wrote it down and fixed it. The gist was meaningful for me because it was the first time I'd seen a serious AI researcher describe the shape of the thing I'd independently been building. That's a useful validation when you've been doing something alone for a while and wondering whether anyone else sees what you see.

Garry Tan's GBrain ships a similar shape for his own use. It's cool. It's also built for one person (him) with no real privacy posture, because he doesn't need one. I've been building for an audience of people inside a company whose whole brand is user-owned and privacy-respecting, which means I've had to make choices he hasn't.

OpenClaw and Hermes are doing the "proactive harness" thing for engineer audiences. They're great at demonstrating how much more you can get out of a model when the scaffolding around it is good. What they don't solve is that 80% of users are not going to live in a terminal.

What I read in all of this is that a lot of us, independently, are figuring out the same lessons at the same time. The harness matters more than the model. Trust has to be structural, not advisory. Corrections have to stick. Memory has to persist across sessions. Privacy has to be architectural, not policy. Nobody has yet put these pieces together for someone who doesn't want to see a terminal.

## What I'm still sitting with

I don't have clean answers to any of the following. I think that's what a month like RSM is for.

**Is what I've built general, or is it secretly CS-shaped?** I think the underlying mechanism is domain-neutral. Claims about entities, trust scoring, provenance, correction durability. Those feel universal to me. But I've only applied them to CS work. If this were to help a project lead or an engineer or a designer, I don't yet know what the shape of that is. Another team taking a hard look would tell me faster than I can figure out alone.

**Is local-first privacy a commercial dead end?** Consumer AI for home life doesn't seem to pay. Work AI does. I don't know whether the architecture I've been forced into by privacy concerns is compatible with the work-tool business model people pay for. Might be. Might not be. I have guesses. None are tested.

**Will it survive its first non-builder user?** I've never handed DailyOS to anyone who didn't build it. The UI is probably stranger than I realise. The install path is probably harder than I think. I haven't earned the right to claim otherwise.

**Is the stuff I've been building better thought of as a product, or as a set of primitives other products could adopt?** If it's the second, me.sh and Day One and Gravatar and Jetpack AI and others could each get memory, trust, and provenance without rebuilding them from scratch. If it's the first, DailyOS is just DailyOS. I don't know which is more useful for Automattic, and I don't want to pre-decide.

## What I want to do next month

Four things, in order of what matters most to me:

1. **Make DailyOS stable enough to hand to a curious Automattician.** Not a team pilot. One or two people who want to try it, with me there to fix what breaks.
2. **Write down what I've learned.** Memory, trust, provenance, corrections, the line between deterministic code and probabilistic AI. In a form another engineer could pick up, not a form I keep in my head.
3. **Have one conversation with another Automattic product team.** The me.sh team has asked to meet; that's the obvious first one. Maybe one more after.
4. **Publish something.** Internally, at least. The patterns I've been finding are broader than my own use, and keeping them in my personal repo isn't helping anyone.

What I'd like: one or two partners for the month. Someone with Rust and Tauri comfort who wants to work on the substrate lanes with me. Or a designer willing to wrestle with the harder problem of showing trust and sources on-screen without turning every output into a wall of footnotes. Or someone who just wants to be the second person using the tool and tell me where it hurts.

What I'm not asking for: headcount, budget, a product-launch decision, a pre-conclusion about what this is before the month is done.

## What could come out of it

I want to be hedged here because I genuinely don't know.

It's possible the people I hand it to keep using it after the month, and we have a real conversation about what it becomes. It's possible the primitives get documented, and another Automattic team picks a piece of them up, and we have a different kind of conversation about shared AI infrastructure. It's possible first contact with a non-builder user reveals what's broken, and we learn where three months of effort went to the wrong place. It's possible none of those happen, and I've had a month of concentrated work on something I'd have done anyway, and Automattic has a clearer picture of what is and isn't here.

Any of those would be a better outcome than the default, which is "James keeps building in his spare time while the category fills up around him." The public conversation is catching up to where I am. I'd like to be slightly ahead of it, with a little help, for one month, and see what that's worth.

I'm one person. I don't know what I don't know. I'd like to find out with some company.
